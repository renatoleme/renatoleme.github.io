Introduction

In 1932, Gödel published a brief note demonstrating that the intuitionistic propositional calculus (IPC) cannot be characterized by finite-valued semantics. Although the argument he employed was brilliant, it was presented in an extremely concise manner (merely two paragraphs!), making it challenging to comprehend. In the subsequent discussion, I will endeavor to present it in a more detailed fashion, addressing any gaps with additional or alternative definitions and arguments. Following Gödel's lead, we will denote the intuitionistic propositional calculus as H\textbf{H}H.

The strategy

Suppose there is a matrix for L\mathcal{L}L which validates those, and only those, formulas that are provable in H\textbf{H}H. More formally, we are interested in a finite-valued matrix M\mathcal{M}M such that,

∀φ∈L,M⊩φ  ⟺  H⊢φ\forall \varphi \in \mathcal{L}, \mathcal{M} \Vdash \varphi \iff \textbf{H} \vdash \varphi∀φ∈L,M⊩φ⟺H⊢φ

The right-to-left direction ("those") is trivial: just take the usual matrix for classical logic. We are interested in the left-to-right ("only those") direction, which is equivalent to:

¬∃φ∈L,M⊩φ∧H⊬φ\neg \exists \varphi \in \mathcal{L}, \mathcal{M} \Vdash \varphi \land \textbf{H} \nvdash \varphi¬∃φ∈L,M⊩φ∧H⊬φ

Therefore, if we find a formula φ\varphiφ valid in M\mathcal{M}M and show that this formula is not provable in H\textbf{H}H, we are forced to reject M\mathcal{M}M as an adequate characterization of H\textbf{H}H. The strategy of Gödel's argument will be to show that, given any finite-valued matrix strong enough to validate every formula provable in H\textbf{H}H, this matrix will necessarily validate also a formula not provable in H\textbf{H}H, implying that no finite-valued matrix can satisfy only the formulas provable in intuitionistic propositional calculus.
